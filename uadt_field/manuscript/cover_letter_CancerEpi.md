Dear Editor-in-Chief,

We submit our manuscript, **"Field Cancerization in the Upper Aerodigestive Tract: Co-occurrence Patterns, Bidirectional Transitions, and Survival Impact in a Population-Based Taiwanese Cohort,"** for consideration as an original research article in *Cancer Epidemiology*.

UADT field cancerization is clinically important yet poorly quantified at the population level, particularly in betel-nut-endemic settings where carcinogen exposure is uniquely intense. Analysing 17 years of population-based registry data from 10,113 UADT cancer patients in Taiwan, we find that one in four pyriform sinus patients develops a second esophageal primary (SIR=3.78, 95% CI 2.40–5.68), with hypopharynx–esophagus as the second-highest pair. Bidirectional, predominantly synchronous site-to-site transitions (symmetry score 0.879, binomial *p*=0.70) confirm shared field-wide carcinogenesis rather than loco-regional spread. After rigorous immortal-time correction via time-varying Cox regression, multi-site UADT disease carries a 2.14-fold mortality excess — an effect that was paradoxically reversed (apparent HR=0.86) in the uncorrected landmark analysis. Competing-risks modelling shows that the actionable surveillance window is concentrated in the first 1–2 years, before the 56.7% five-year mortality depletes the at-risk population.

This work is well suited to *Cancer Epidemiology* because it addresses cancer co-occurrence, population-level risk quantification, and a methodological contribution (immortal-time bias quantification) directly relevant to retrospective registry research — core topics of the journal. The Taiwan context, with its unusually high betel nut exposure prevalence, provides a high-signal population for studying a mechanism of general epidemiological relevance.

This manuscript has not been published and is not under consideration elsewhere. All authors have approved the final version.
