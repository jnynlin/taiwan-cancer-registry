We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"Convergent Unsupervised Machine
Learning Reveals an Aerodigestive Field-Cancerization Domain Driving Multiple Primary
Cancers in Taiwan"** for consideration as an Original Research Article in *Cancer
Epidemiology*. This work has not been published previously, is not under consideration
elsewhere, and all authors have approved the submitted version.

Multiple primary cancers are an increasingly important clinical problem, yet the patterns
by which specific cancers co-occur within a patient — and the shared mechanisms they imply —
remain poorly characterized, particularly in Asian populations. Using the full Taiwan Cancer
Registry (78,578 patients, 2006–2020), we applied three independent unsupervised
machine-learning methods — association rule mining, non-negative matrix factorization, and a
deep autoencoder with clustering — to discover co-existing cancer patterns without prior
hypotheses, and corroborated them with standardized incidence ratios (SIR) and directional
disease-trajectory mining. Our central finding is that all three orthogonal unsupervised
methods converged on the same three co-occurrence domains, with an aerodigestive
squamous-cell-carcinoma field (esophagus, oral cavity, hypopharynx, larynx) emerging as the
dominant driver: 96% male, youngest onset (median 55 years), highest mortality (68.8%), and a
13% rate of a third primary cancer — far exceeding the cohort-wide multiple-primary rate of
5.2%. SIR analysis quantified up to 12-fold excess second-primary risk within this field, and
trajectory mining showed these aerodigestive pairs arise synchronously (60% within 6 months),
in contrast to the unidirectional, metachronous breast and colorectal second primaries.

We believe this work is well suited to *Cancer Epidemiology* for three reasons. First, it
addresses second-primary-cancer risk — a core theme of the journal — at population scale.
Second, the methodological convergence across three distinct algorithms provides unusually
strong evidence that the discovered domains are biologically real rather than analytic
artifacts, an approach we believe will interest the journal's methodologically-minded
readership. Third, the finding carries a direct, actionable clinical implication: patients
presenting with any aerodigestive squamous-cell carcinoma warrant intensified, field-directed
pan-aerodigestive surveillance.
