Abstract

Objectives. The upper aerodigestive tract (UADT) is exposed to shared carcinogens — betel nut, tobacco, and alcohol — creating conditions for field cancerization, in which mucosal exposure produces independent primary tumours at multiple sites. Taiwan's high prevalence of betel nut chewing offers a unique population to study field cancerization epidemiology. We aimed to quantify UADT co-occurrence rates, characterise the directionality of site-to-site transitions, determine the survival impact of multi-site UADT disease, and demonstrate the magnitude of immortal-time bias as a methodological warning for retrospective multi-primary cancer analyses.

Materials and Methods. We analysed a population-based cancer registry spanning 2003–2020 comprising 10,113 patients with at least one cancer at a predefined UADT field site (ICD-O C02–C06, C09–C10, C12–C13, C15). Standardised incidence ratios (SIR) quantified the elevation in second-primary risk. Synchronous versus metachronous transitions were characterised by a symmetry score. Survival impact was estimated using time-varying Cox regression to eliminate immortal-time bias, and validated with a competing-risks DeepHit neural network model.

Results. Among 10,113 UADT field patients, 1,067 (10.6%) developed ≥ 2 field sites. Pyriform sinus patients had the highest absolute second-primary rate (24.2% co-occurring with esophagus; SIR = 3.78, 95% CI 2.40–5.68) followed by hypopharynx–esophagus (16.4%; SIR = 2.86, 95% CI 1.96–4.04). Transitions were bidirectional and predominantly synchronous (60% within 6 months; symmetry score 0.879, binomial p = 0.70), consistent with shared field exposure rather than metastasis. After immortal-time correction, multi-site disease carried a hazard ratio for death of 2.14 (95% CI 1.97–2.33, p < 0.001). Competing-risks analysis confirmed 5-year cumulative incidence of second UADT cancer of 10.4% against a backdrop of 56.7% all-cause mortality, underscoring the urgency of early surveillance.

Conclusions. One in four pyriform sinus patients develops a second esophageal primary. Bidirectional, predominantly synchronous transitions and a 2.14-fold mortality excess confirm a field cancerization mechanism. Routine early esophagoscopic surveillance within 1–2 years of a UADT diagnosis is warranted, particularly for pyriform sinus and hypopharynx primaries.

Methods overview

Co-occurrence & SIR

90 ordered site pairs · Poisson person-years model · Sex × 5-year age-band stratification · Expected counts from general registry (excluding UADT patients) · BH FDR correction

Transition directionality

Synchronous = ≤ 6 months (sensitivity: 3 mo, 9 mo) · Symmetry score = min/max of forward/reverse counts · Binomial test for directional departure · Pairs with n ≥ 10 transitions

Survival — Time-varying Cox

lifelines package · multi_field = 0 before 2nd dx date, 1 after · Eliminates immortal-time bias by construction · 6-month landmark retained for comparison (ΔHR reported)

Competing risks — DeepHit

Neural network, 2 cause-specific heads (death / 2nd UADT cancer) · Shared trunk d = 64, 2 layers · NLL + ranking loss (α = 0.2) · 30 percentile-based time bins · C-index ≈ 0.60

Novelty context vs published literature

Closest existing paper: Huang et al. 2009 (BMC Cancer) — SIR for second esophageal primary in oral/pharyngeal patients, same Taiwan registry, earlier cohort (~1979–2003). Our paper extends and differentiates in four ways:

  • Post-betel-regulation cohort (2003–2020 vs pre-2003) — captures the regulatory era
  • C12 pyriform sinus specificity — 24.2% is the headline number; Huang reports overall pharyngeal SIR, not C12-specific rates
  • Survival modeling with immortal-time correction — ΔHR = 1.28 is a new methodological contribution not in Huang
  • Competing-risks DeepHit — no prior Taiwan UADT study uses neural competing-risks to quantify the death-vs-surveillance race

Western comparators: Haughey 1992, Schwartz 2007 report 3–8% second esophageal primary rates in head & neck cohorts. Our 24.2% (3–5× higher) directly reflects betel nut triple-carcinogen exposure unique to Taiwan.
